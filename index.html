Predicting influenza vaccination distribution using search engine behavior

A capstone project by Ashley Wheeler, Tien Nguyen, Jackie Glosson, and Andrew Chen

Project Dashboard

Background

Each year, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) dedicates millions of dollars of funding to measure, predict, and mitigate the spread of influenza throughout the population. In recent years, search engine behavior has proved to be a viable tool in detecting when influenza spreads uptick (Ginsberg et al., 2009). As the disease spreads, individuals are more likely to search for their symptoms, leaving a traceable digital footprint. This project sought to utlize google search engine behaviore to predict influenza vaccination distribution. The project was broken into two separate portions: (1) search engine behavior was forecasted for the upcoming (2022-2023) flu season based on historical data (2) Search engine predictions and additional factors (demographic makeup, political affiliation, temperature) were used to predict influenza vaccine distribution for the upcoming (2022-2023) flu season.

Interpolation & Concatenation

CONCATENATION We concatenated a total of 22 datasets together.

MISSING DATA There were multiple instances of missing data. Median houshold income, bachelor's attainment, and racial composition were all missing for the year of 2021, as census data postings were delayed from the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic. Two interpolation methods were tested, with the result method being a combination of linear regression and interpolation.

INTERPOLATION Most of our data sources were not interspaced at monthly time intervals, thus data had to be interpolated. For example, all data collected from the census website was collected annually or bi-annually, therefore monthly values had to be approximated.

Distributions and descriptive statistics were compared before and after interpolation for each variable. Metrics were found to be similar, with no drastic compositional changes.

Exploratory Data Analysis

GOOGLE QUERY SELECTION The google trends search term “FLU SHOT” has notably the largest standard deviation (or largest variance) of all search terms. We can know why this is by learning how the google trends data is obtained. Google trends data normalizes data with respect to the queries (maximum of five) examined together. In this instance, the query of “FLU SHOT” was searched more times than any other query overall – we know this because google trends assigns a maximum value of 100 to the data point representing the most significant number of searches for that period of time and group of queries. We can also detect that the term “FLU SHOT” was searched the most from the mean. When we consider which search query to use in our model, we will want to take this into account, as the other search queries may not capture as much variance as our FLU SHOT query since the way it is normalized through google trends “compresses” the other queries. Therefore we will select the “FLU SHOT” variable for our google trends variable.

DATA VARIANCE Overall, we see minimal variance (small standard deviation) in our demographic variables. Minimal variance is expected, as these values within states change very little over time- perhaps only a few decimal places. For example, PERCENT_OVER_65, WHITE, BLACK, PERCENT_VOTED_DEMOCRATIC, and PERCENT_VOTED_REPUBLICAN all have standard deviations of less than one.

CORRELATIONS We observe that the only variables strongly correlated with VAX_PERCENT_DISTRIBUTION were google trends variables. All other variables demonstrate weak or no correlation to this target variable.

Predicting Search Query Behavior

ALGORITHMS EXAMINED Three seperate algorithms were tested on a single state's data: SARIMA, RNN LSTM, and RNN. Algorithm performance was compared, and SARIMA was decided to be the algorithm of best fit based on its comparatively lower root mean squared error (RMSE= 14.1) and higher R-squared value (R2=0.77). SARIMA also had the most consistent performance when applied across states.

SARIMA Seasonal Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA) is a time series forecasting algorithm. In many time series data, seasonal effects come into play so there is a cyclical rise and fall or seasonality cycles in the data. SARIMA takes the seasonality cycles into consideration to predict future values.
